\begin{abstract}
Recent work has demonstrated that water-filled head-mounted displays (HMDs)
can achieve neutral buoyancy, making active swimming in underwater VR physically
feasible~\cite{osone2017safe,osone2018optimized}.
However, once users actually swim forward, the boxy shell of conventional HMDs
imposes substantial hydrodynamic drag and, during head rotation, an abrupt
restoring torque—both of which translate directly to cervical fatigue
and degraded experience quality.
We present the \textit{Hammerhead HMD}, a water-filled underwater AR headset
whose outer shell is shaped after the cephalophoil of hammerhead sharks
(\textit{Sphyrna} spp.).
The lateral extension delays flow separation at 0° yaw,
and generates a stable leading-edge vortex (LEV) at yaw angles up to 30°,
smoothly redirecting flow and reducing neck torque.
We evaluate both effects using Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV)
and compare quantitatively against a conventional box-shaped baseline.

%%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
%%------------------------------------------------------------

Osone et al.~\cite{osone2017safe,osone2018optimized} established a key
breakthrough: filling the optical cavity with water equalizes internal
and external pressure, eliminating the positive buoyancy that had
previously pulled users toward the surface and strained their necks.
Their system evaluation confirms that users can perform head movements
comfortably \textit{while stationary}.

Yet as soon as a user begins to swim forward, a different physics regime
takes over: hydrodynamic drag.
A flat-fronted, box-shaped HMD behaves as a bluff body, generating
a large separated wake and substantial pressure drag.
During yaw rotation—turning the head to look sideways—the same box profile
stalls abruptly and produces a sharp restoring torque that resists the motion.
Neither effect was measured in prior work.

We address this gap with the \textbf{Hammerhead HMD}, whose shell geometry
is directly inspired by the cephalophoil of hammerhead sharks.
This lateral cranial extension has been studied as a source of enhanced
binocular overlap and hydrodynamic control surface~\cite{kajiura2003}.
Translated to an HMD, it (i) reduces the frontal bluff-body cross-section,
(ii) widens the stereo camera baseline for improved depth estimation in water,
and (iii) produces a stable LEV during yaw, reducing neck torque.

A high-speed camera acquired tracer images at \textbf{4{,}000\,fps}.

characterization, a \textbf{stereo PIV} configuration was employed.

Velocity fields were computed using PIVlab~\cite{thielicke2021pivlab};
pressure fields were reconstructed via the Poisson equation.
